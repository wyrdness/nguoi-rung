## Overview

Người Rừng (Vietnamese for 'forest man') is a proposed hominid cryptid reported from the rugged forests of Vietnam and Laos, with related accounts extending into northern Borneo. The same or related figure is also called Batutut and Ujit in cryptozoological literature, and was nicknamed 'rock ape' by U.S. servicemen during the Vietnam War. Witnesses describe an upright, hair-covered being roughly six feet tall, completely covered with grey, brown, or black hair except on the knees, the soles of the feet, the hands, and the face. Reports describe both solitary individuals and small groups. Long before American military involvement, Vietnamese, Lao, and Montagnard highland communities maintained a substantial oral tradition of forest people seen at the edges of clearings and remote settlements. Modern scientific engagement begins with British primatologist John MacKinnon, who reported observing unusual bipedal tracks in 1970 during fieldwork that he later described in his 1975 book In Search of the Red Ape; MacKinnon at the time speculated about a hominid related to Meganthropus. Professor Trần Hồng Việt of the Pedagogic University of Hanoi announced in 1982 that he had found and cast similar footprints, measuring 28 × 16 cm, comparable to MacKinnon's 1970 observations. No specimen has been recovered, and no peer-reviewed evidence has demonstrated that the Người Rừng exists; mainstream zoologists treat reports as folkloric and as plausibly explained by misidentification of known primates (gibbons, langurs, and other apes) and bears.

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Undescribed Indochinese hominid** _(John MacKinnon (early speculation), Trần Hồng Việt)_

Hypothesis that the Người Rừng represents an undescribed bipedal hominid species, possibly related to Meganthropus, surviving in remote Annamite forests.

### Skeptical

**Misidentification of known primates** _(Mainstream zoology)_

Skeptical view that Người Rừng reports represent misidentified gibbons, langurs, macaques, and possibly Asian black bears, particularly under stressful wartime or low-light conditions.

**No biological evidence** _(Mainstream zoology)_

No specimen, body, bone, or unambiguous genetic evidence has been produced; mainstream zoology treats the Người Rừng as folkloric.
